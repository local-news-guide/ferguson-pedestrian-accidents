Alabama Pedestrian Law

Know Your Rights as a Pedestrian in Alabama

⚖ Key Alabama Pedestrian Laws

  • Drivers must yield to pedestrians in marked and unmarked crosswalks (Ala. Code § 32-5A-210)
  • Pedestrians have the right-of-way at signalized intersections when the walk signal is active
  • Drivers must exercise due care to avoid colliding with any pedestrian on any roadway (Ala. Code § 32-5A-213)
  • Vehicles stopped at a crosswalk must not be overtaken or passed by other drivers (Ala. Code § 32-5A-211)
  • Drivers must yield the right-of-way to blind pedestrians carrying a white cane or accompanied by a guide dog (Ala. Code § 32-5A-220)
  • If a sidewalk is available, pedestrians must use it rather than the adjacent roadway (Ala. Code § 32-5A-215)
  • Alabama follows contributory negligence — if you are found even 1% at fault, you could lose your entire claim
  • Alabama requires minimum bodily injury liability coverage of $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident
  • Hit-and-run drivers face criminal charges, and you may still recover through uninsured motorist coverage
  • The statute of limitations for personal injury in Alabama is 2 years from the date of the accident

Children deserve better protection. When a driver strikes a child in a school zone or on a residential road, the consequences are devastating. Ferguson & Ferguson holds negligent drivers fully accountable.

After an Accident

What to Do After a Pedestrian Accident in Garden City

  1. Call 911 Immediately

    Call 911. Garden City Police or Cullman County Sheriff will respond. If a child is involved, request EMS immediately regardless of apparent injury severity.

  2. Seek Medical Attention

    Take your child or yourself to Cullman Regional Medical Center. Children are especially vulnerable to head injuries and internal trauma from pedestrian impacts.

  3. Document Everything

    Photograph the scene, the vehicle, injuries, road conditions, school zone signage, and any lack of crosswalks. Get contact information from witnesses.

  4. Do Not Give Recorded Statements

    The driver's insurance company will call quickly. They want statements to twist against you under Alabama's contributory negligence rule. Politely decline and refer them to your attorney.

Pedestrian Accident FAQ — Garden City, Alabama

My child was hit in a school zone near Garden City Elementary. What are our options?

Drivers owe an elevated duty of care in school zones. If a driver was speeding, distracted, or failed to yield to your child in a school zone, they may be liable for all medical costs, pain and suffering, and other damages. Alabama allows parents to file claims on behalf of minor children. Contact Ferguson & Ferguson immediately to protect your family's rights.

What makes Garden City dangerous for pedestrians?

Garden City is growing rapidly, but pedestrian safety infrastructure hasn't kept pace. Most streets lack sidewalks, school zones near Garden City Elementary have limited crossing protections, and increasing traffic volumes on roads designed for rural use create more pedestrian-vehicle conflicts every year.

Can I file a claim if my child was walking to a bus stop and was hit by a car?

Yes. Children walking to bus stops are among the most vulnerable pedestrians. Drivers are expected to watch for children near school bus stops, especially in residential areas. When sidewalks don't exist and children must walk along roads, the driver's duty of care is even greater. Ferguson & Ferguson handles school zone and bus stop pedestrian accident cases.

How does Alabama's contributory negligence law affect a child's pedestrian accident claim?

Alabama courts recognize that children cannot be held to the same standard as adults when it comes to contributory negligence. A child's age, experience, and judgment are considered. Young children may not be found contributorily negligent at all. This is a critical legal distinction that can make or break a claim.

What compensation is available for child pedestrian accident victims?

Compensation may include medical bills (current and future), rehabilitation costs, pain and suffering, emotional trauma, loss of childhood activities, and in severe cases, permanent disability. Parents may also recover damages for their own emotional distress and the cost of caring for an injured child.
