Dodge City's Development Boom Creates Pedestrian Hazards

Dodge City is experiencing rapid residential growth, with new subdivisions bringing hundreds of families to this small Cullman County community. But with a population of just 600, the town's infrastructure hasn't kept pace.

Your Legal Rights

Alabama Pedestrian Accident Law

Contributory Negligence Rule

Alabama follows a strict contributory negligence standard. If you're found even 1% at fault for your accident, you cannot recover compensation. Insurance companies exploit this rule by blaming pedestrians—even when drivers or property owners were primarily responsible.

We counter these tactics. Our team gathers evidence showing the defendant's full responsibility: construction zone violations, lack of required safety infrastructure, speeding data, or failure to maintain safe conditions in development zones.

Relevant Alabama Code Sections

  • Ala. Code § 32-5A-220: Pedestrian right-of-way in crosswalks
  • Ala. Code § 32-5A-221: Pedestrian crossing at locations other than crosswalks
  • Ala. Code § 32-5A-222: Pedestrians on roadways without sidewalks
  • Ala. Code § 6-5-410: Two-year statute of limitations for personal injury claims

Two-Year Deadline to File

You have two years from the date of your pedestrian accident to file a lawsuit in Alabama. Evidence degrades, witnesses' memories fade, and construction zones change rapidly. The sooner you contact Ferguson & Ferguson, the stronger your case.

Protect Your Claim

What to Do After a Pedestrian Accident in Dodge City

1

Get Medical Care Immediately

Call 911 or have someone drive you to Cullman Regional Medical Center. Pedestrian injuries—even those that seem minor—can worsen quickly. Documented medical treatment creates a record linking your injuries to the accident.

2

Report the Accident

File a report with local law enforcement. If the accident occurred in a construction zone or development area, request that officers document road conditions, missing safety features, and any violations of construction site requirements.

3

Document Everything

Take photos of the accident scene, your injuries, lack of sidewalks or lighting, construction equipment, road conditions, and any hazards. Get contact information from witnesses. If possible, note the name of the development or construction company working in the area.

4

Don't Talk to Insurance Adjusters

Insurers for drivers, contractors, or developers will try to get you to give recorded statements or accept quick settlements. Politely decline until you've spoken with an attorney. Their goal is to minimize your payout—ours is to maximize it.
