Alabama Pedestrian Law

Know Your Rights as a Pedestrian in Alabama

⚖ Key Alabama Pedestrian Laws

  • Drivers must yield to pedestrians in marked and unmarked crosswalks (Ala. Code § 32-5A-210)
  • Pedestrians have the right-of-way at signalized intersections when the walk signal is active
  • Drivers must exercise due care to avoid colliding with any pedestrian on any roadway (Ala. Code § 32-5A-213)
  • Vehicles stopped at a crosswalk must not be overtaken or passed by other drivers (Ala. Code § 32-5A-211)
  • Drivers must yield the right-of-way to blind pedestrians carrying a white cane or accompanied by a guide dog (Ala. Code § 32-5A-220)
  • If a sidewalk is available, pedestrians must use it rather than the adjacent roadway (Ala. Code § 32-5A-215)
  • Alabama follows contributory negligence — if you are found even 1% at fault, you could lose your entire claim
  • Alabama requires minimum bodily injury liability coverage of $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident
  • Hit-and-run drivers face criminal charges, and you may still recover through uninsured motorist coverage
  • The statute of limitations for personal injury in Alabama is 2 years from the date of the accident

After an Accident

What to Do After a Pedestrian Accident in Hanceville

  1. Call 911 Immediately

    Get medical help and create an official police report. Hanceville Police Department will respond and document the scene — this report is critical evidence for your case.

  2. Get Medical Treatment

    Go to Cullman Regional Medical Center or your doctor immediately. Pedestrian impact injuries — head trauma, internal bleeding, spinal injuries — often don't show symptoms for hours. Early documentation links your injuries to the accident.

  3. Document the Scene

    Photograph the location, your injuries, the vehicle, traffic signals, and any lack of crosswalks or sidewalks. Near Wallace State, capture the campus context — parking lot configurations, pedestrian traffic patterns, and lighting conditions.

  4. Do Not Give Recorded Statements

    The driver's insurance company will call quickly. They want statements they can use against you under contributory negligence. Politely decline and refer them to your attorney.

Frequently Asked Questions

Pedestrian Accident FAQ — Hanceville, Alabama

I was hit near Wallace State campus. Can I still file a claim if I was crossing outside a crosswalk?

Yes — but Alabama's contributory negligence law makes it complicated. Insurance companies will argue that crossing outside a marked crosswalk makes you partially at fault, which could bar your entire claim. However, in areas like Wallace State where there are no marked crosswalks or pedestrian signals, drivers still owe a duty of care. Ferguson & Ferguson builds cases that prove the driver's negligence was the cause of the accident, not your walking path.

What injuries are most common in Hanceville pedestrian accidents?

Pedestrian accidents near Wallace State and along US-31 commonly result in traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, broken bones (especially legs, hips, and pelvis), internal organ damage, road rash and soft tissue injuries, and concussions. The severity depends on vehicle speed — US-31 highway-speed impacts tend to be far more catastrophic than lower-speed parking lot incidents.

Absolutely. Ferguson & Ferguson works on a contingency fee basis — you pay nothing upfront and nothing at all unless we win your case. This is especially important for college students who may not have resources to pay hourly legal fees. Our consultation is free, and our fee comes from a percentage of the compensation we recover for you.

How does contributory negligence apply to pedestrian accidents near a college campus?

Alabama's contributory negligence rule means insurance companies will try to blame you for wearing earbuds, looking at your phone, crossing outside marked areas, or even walking at night near campus. If they succeed in proving any fault on your part, your claim could be denied entirely. An experienced attorney knows how to counter these arguments by proving the driver had the greater duty of care and failed to meet it.

What if the driver who hit me in Hanceville was uninsured or fled the scene?

You may still recover compensation through your own uninsured/underinsured motorist (UM/UIM) coverage. Alabama law requires insurance companies to offer UM/UIM coverage, and many drivers carry it without realizing it. Hit-and-run drivers also face criminal charges under Alabama law. Ferguson & Ferguson investigates all potential sources of recovery.

How long do I have to file a pedestrian accident lawsuit in Alabama?

Alabama's statute of limitations is 2 years from the date of the accident. Missing this deadline means losing your right to file a lawsuit. Because building a strong case requires time to preserve evidence and document injuries, we recommend contacting a lawyer as soon as possible after your accident.
